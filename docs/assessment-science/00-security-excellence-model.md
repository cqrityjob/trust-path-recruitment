# Document 00 — Security Excellence Model

**Status:** Foundational. Every other document in this set (01–10) derives from this one.
**Scope note:** written at the level of professional excellence in high-consequence, trust-dependent work generally — recruitment, career guidance, annual review, promotion, supplier validation, and every future CQrityjob intelligence capability inherits from this document, not only "assessment" narrowly.

## 1. The question this document answers

Before any competency list, dimension, question, or score: **what
objectively distinguishes an outstanding security professional from
an average one, and an average one from someone who should not hold
the role at all?**

This is deliberately not the same question as "what does a security
job description ask for." Job descriptions describe tasks. Excellence
is about *how reliably and how well* those tasks are performed under
real conditions — including the conditions that matter most: fatigue,
ambiguity, social pressure, boredom, and moments where nothing appears
to be happening until suddenly something is.

Security work belongs to a specific class of occupations studied
extensively in the literature on **high-reliability organizations
(HRO)** — nuclear operations, aviation, maritime, air traffic control,
emergency medicine — where the defining feature is not average
performance but the *absence of catastrophic failure* over long
stretches of low-stimulation, high-vigilance work, punctuated by rare
moments that demand near-perfect judgement. Weick and Sutcliffe's work
on "mindful organizing" and Reason's research on organizational
accidents both converge on the same finding: the professionals who
prevent disasters are not distinguished primarily by raw intelligence
or technical skill, but by a specific cluster of *attentional and
judgement habits* — sustained noticing, reluctance to simplify,
sensitivity to operations, deference to expertise regardless of
hierarchy, and commitment to resilience when something does go wrong.
This document treats security work as a member of that occupational
class, not as generic customer-facing or compliance labor, because
that classification is what determines which traits actually predict
outcomes.

## 2. What excellence looks like, by tier

### Top 5% — the professionals organizations build resilience around

- **Sustained vigilance without cue-dependence.** Excellent performers
  do not need an alarm, an instruction, or a visible incident to
  remain attentive; their attention operates on a self-generated
  schedule, not an externally triggered one. This is the single
  clearest behavioral marker separating top performers from adequate
  ones in vigilance-task research (the broader "sustained attention"
  literature going back to Mackworth's original vigilance studies,
  replicated extensively in air-traffic-control and CCTV-monitoring
  research).
- **Calibrated escalation.** They escalate exactly as much as a
  situation warrants — neither under-reacting (normalization of
  deviance, the pattern implicated in the Challenger and Columbia
  disasters per Vaughan's analysis) nor over-reacting in a way that
  erodes their own credibility and trains colleagues to ignore them.
  This calibration is learned, not innate, but it correlates with a
  stable underlying trait: low tolerance for unexplained anomalies.
- **Judgement under incomplete information.** Naturalistic
  decision-making research (Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision model)
  shows that expert judgement in time-pressured, ambiguous conditions
  is pattern-recognition built from deep, varied exposure, not
  slower-but-more-careful analytical reasoning. Top performers make
  fast, usually-correct calls and can articulate *why* after the fact
  — the articulation matters because it is evidence the judgement was
  structured, not lucky.
- **Documentation as a safety behavior, not paperwork.** In HRO
  contexts, the professionals who write things down promptly and
  precisely are disproportionately the ones who catch their own
  near-misses and enable others to learn from them. Documentation
  quality is a genuine excellence marker, not an administrative
  afterthought.
- **Composure that holds under social and physical threat.** Distinct
  from general "stress tolerance" — this is specifically the ability
  to keep executing a correct procedure while being shouted at,
  physically challenged, or facing status threats, without either
  freezing or escalating disproportionately.
- **Ethical steadiness when unobserved.** The reliability of a
  professional's conduct when no one is checking is one of the most
  consistently cited (and hardest to assess) markers of true integrity
  in the personnel-selection literature — self-report measures of
  integrity are known to be weak here; behavioral history and
  situational judgement evidence are stronger (see Document 04).

### Top 20% — strong, dependable, promotable

Reliable execution of procedure, good but not exceptional pattern
recognition (needs more time or more confirmation than the top tier),
solid documentation, good composure most of the time with occasional
lapses under compounding stressors, sound ethical judgement with
occasional need for a second opinion on genuinely ambiguous calls.
This tier is where the majority of a well-selected workforce sits, and
where most development investment has the best return — the gap
between top-20% and top-5% is largely experience and calibrated
feedback, not a different kind of person (see §4).

### Minimum acceptable competence

A floor, not a target: follows procedure without being reminded in the
moment it matters; reports what they see rather than filtering it
through what they think a supervisor wants to hear; does not
fabricate or embellish records; can be trusted with the specific level
of authority the role carries (e.g. use of force, access control
decisions, information handling) without requiring constant
supervision; recognizes the limits of their own authority and
competence and escalates rather than improvising outside them. Falling
below this floor on *any single one* of these is a disqualifying
signal regardless of strength elsewhere — this is a deliberately
non-compensatory principle, discussed further in Document 06.

## 3. The core construct cluster

Synthesizing the above, five constructs recur across the HRO, CRM,
military/police-selection, and personnel-psychology literatures as the
ones that actually differentiate outcomes in trust-dependent,
high-consequence roles — not an exhaustive list of "nice traits," but
the ones with real predictive weight:

1. **Reliability** — doing the right thing the same way, every time,
   including when no one is watching and when it is boring.
2. **Professional judgement** — calibrated decision-making under
   ambiguity and incomplete information; knowing what you don't know.
3. **Ethical steadiness** — behavior under conditions where cutting a
   corner would go undetected.
4. **Decision quality under pressure** — not speed alone, and not
   caution alone, but the appropriate balance for the specific stakes
   of the moment (this is where Crew Resource Management's concept of
   "workload management" is directly relevant: excellent performers
   protect their own decision quality by managing what they attend to,
   rather than trying to process everything at once).
5. **Adaptability with fidelity** — adjusting to novel situations
   without abandoning the underlying principle the procedure exists to
   protect. This is distinct from rigid rule-following (which fails
   novel situations) and from improvisation (which abandons the
   safety principle) — the excellent performer adapts the *method*
   while preserving the *intent*.

A sixth, cross-cutting construct — **continuous learning** — is not a
performance construct in the same sense as the five above; it is the
mechanism by which a professional moves from top-20% toward top-5%
over a career, and the primary lever available to development
programs. It is treated separately in §4.

## 4. Trainable vs. largely fixed

This distinction is load-bearing for the entire platform: it
determines what a Requirement Profile or a development plan can
realistically ask an organization to select for versus develop for,
and what an assessment can fairly hold against a candidate.

**Realistically trainable, with structured practice and feedback:**
procedural knowledge; documentation quality and habits; de-escalation
technique; specific decision protocols (e.g. structured triage,
checklists); situational-awareness *technique* (scanning patterns,
deliberate noticing habits) even where baseline vigilance capacity
varies; communication clarity; most technical/system skills;
calibration of escalation thresholds through experience and feedback.
This is consistent with the broader finding in training-effectiveness
research that skills with a clear correct procedure and available
feedback loops respond well to training; the effect sizes are among
the more robust in applied psychology.

**Difficult or slow to develop, with real ceiling effects:**
underlying conscientiousness and impulse control (among the more
temporally stable personality dimensions across adulthood in
longitudinal trait research); baseline integrity/honesty disposition;
core sustained-attention capacity (technique can compensate somewhat,
but there is a real individual-differences ceiling); composure under
acute threat (some component is trainable via exposure and
inoculation-style training, but a meaningful trait component remains);
deep pattern-recognition expertise, which per Klein's own research
requires years of varied, feedback-rich experience and cannot be
substantially compressed by classroom training alone.

**Practically un-trainable within an employment relationship:** basic
honesty/integrity as a disposition (training can improve *detection*
and *disclosure* behavior around ethical dilemmas, but does not create
integrity where the underlying disposition is absent); willingness to
be held accountable. These belong in selection, not development —
this is why they receive disproportionate weight in the Requirement
Profile / assessment design principles in Document 06, rather than
being treated as coachable gaps.

**Implication for the whole platform:** an assessment result that
conflates "hasn't been trained yet" with "unlikely to ever meet the
bar" is scientifically wrong and operationally harmful — it wastes
development investment on the wrong 20% and under-invests in the
professionals who would respond best to it. Every downstream document
that touches development recommendations (Document 08) or Requirement
Profiles (Document 06/07) must preserve this distinction explicitly,
never collapse it into a single "score."

## 5. Behaviors that predict long-term success vs. future failure

**Predict long-term success** (precursor-behavior framing, consistent
with HRO "weak signal" literature): proactively reports near-misses
involving their own conduct, not just others'; asks clarifying
questions before acting in ambiguous situations rather than guessing;
seeks feedback rather than avoiding it; treats procedure changes as
information rather than as bureaucratic friction; maintains the same
standard whether supervised or not (directly observable via 360-style
or peer-report evidence over time, weakly via self-report alone — see
Document 04); recovers composure quickly after a stressful incident
rather than carrying disrupted judgement into the next task.

**Predict future failure** (the "normalization of deviance" pattern,
per Vaughan's analysis of organizational accidents, and the broader
"drift into failure" literature, e.g. Dekker): a documented history of
successfully cutting a corner without consequence, which — counter to
naive intuition — is one of the *strongest* predictors of a future,
larger failure, because it teaches the professional and their
environment that the deviation is safe; discounting or arguing away
anomalies rather than investigating them; reluctance to report
colleagues' unsafe behavior (bystander suppression, closely tied to
psychological-safety research); escalating conflict rather than
de-escalating when challenged; degraded documentation quality over
time (frequently an early, detectable signal of broader disengagement
or overload, well before a serious incident occurs); a widening gap
between stated procedure and actual practice that the professional
either doesn't notice or doesn't flag.

The practical implication: **a single incident, positive or negative,
is weak evidence.** A *pattern* of these precursor behaviors, observed
or self-reported consistently over time, is strong evidence. This is
the scientific basis for Document 06's requirement that Blueprints
gather multiple, independent pieces of evidence per competency rather
than relying on any single question or exercise.

## 6. What this document deliberately does not do

It does not name specific competencies (Document 02), does not name
specific assessment dimensions (Document 03), does not propose
evidence-collection methods (Document 04), and does not weight
anything by role or environment (Document 07). It establishes the
construct cluster and the trainable/fixed and success/failure
distinctions that all of those documents must trace back to.
