# Document 02 — CQrityjob Competency Framework

**Derives from:** [Document 00](./00-security-excellence-model.md) — every domain and competency below is a decomposition of Document 00 §3's construct cluster (Reliability, Professional Judgement, Ethical Steadiness, Decision Quality Under Pressure, Adaptability with Fidelity, Continuous Learning) into observable, assessable units.
**Scope note:** this is a shared competency vocabulary for every future CQrityjob capability, not an assessment-only artifact — Requirement Profiles, Career Intelligence, workforce mapping, and development recommendations all reference the same competency set.
**Grounding:** grounded in, and intended to extend rather than duplicate, the existing Career Intelligence Graph vocabulary (`cig_competencies`, `cig_skills`, `cig_knowledge_areas`). This document supplies the *rationale layer* — why each competency matters and what it looks like in practice — that the graph's rows never had. No question mapping. No weights.

## How to read this document

Ten domains, each grouping competencies that share an underlying
psychological mechanism (not merely a job-description theme). Every
competency has the same six fields: **Definition**, **Why it
matters**, **Observable behaviours**, **Positive indicators**,
**Warning indicators**, **Development potential** (trainable /
partially trainable / largely fixed, per Document 00 §4 — used
verbatim, not re-litigated per competency).

## Domain A — Operational Vigilance & Situational Awareness

*Mechanism: sustained, self-generated attention and environmental sense-making (Document 00 §2, top-tier marker 1).*

### A1. Sustained Vigilance
**Definition:** the capacity to maintain effective monitoring over
long periods without external cueing.
**Why it matters:** the single most-replicated differentiator between
adequate and excellent performers in monitoring-heavy roles (control
room, CCTV, static post, perimeter).
**Observable behaviours:** consistent scan patterns across a full
shift; noticing changes against a stable baseline rather than only
obvious events; no measurable attention decay in the final hours of a
shift.
**Positive indicators:** proactively varies scanning routine to avoid
habituation; catches subtle anomalies unprompted.
**Warning indicators:** relies on others or on alarms to notice
anything; attention visibly narrows under boredom or fatigue.
**Development potential:** partially trainable (technique/scan
patterns respond to training; underlying sustained-attention capacity
has a real ceiling — Document 00 §4).

### A2. Anomaly Detection
**Definition:** recognizing when something deviates from an expected
baseline, even without being told what to look for.
**Why it matters:** most real incidents are preceded by a detectable
but non-obvious deviation; this is the competency that catches it
before escalation is forced by events.
**Observable behaviours:** flags things that "don't look right" and
can articulate why after investigation; distinguishes signal from
normal variation.
**Positive indicators:** investigates ambiguous cues before dismissing
them; builds an accurate mental baseline quickly in a new environment.
**Warning indicators:** explains away anomalies without checking
(normalization of deviance, Document 00 §5); treats every shift as
identical regardless of context.
**Development potential:** trainable with structured practice and
feedback (deliberate-noticing exercises, after-action review).

### A3. Environmental & Contextual Awareness
**Definition:** understanding the operational context — who should be
where, what is normal for this specific site/time, what external
factors (weather, events, threat level) change the baseline.
**Why it matters:** the same behaviour can be benign or alarming
depending entirely on context; awareness without context produces
false alarms or missed signals.
**Observable behaviours:** references site-specific and situational
context accurately when explaining a decision; updates mental model
when conditions change.
**Positive indicators:** proactively seeks context before a shift
(briefings, handover notes); connects seemingly unrelated observations.
**Warning indicators:** applies identical judgement regardless of
context; ignores handover information.
**Development potential:** trainable (briefing discipline, structured
handover); depth of contextual expertise grows with tenure.

## Domain B — Judgement & Decision-Making

*Mechanism: calibrated, evidence-based decisions under ambiguity and time pressure (Document 00 §2, markers 2–3; §5).*

### B1. Calibrated Escalation
**Definition:** matching the intensity of a response to the actual
severity of a situation — neither under- nor over-reacting.
**Why it matters:** miscalibration in either direction has real cost:
under-escalation risks harm; chronic over-escalation trains others to
discount the professional's judgement (Document 00 §2).
**Observable behaviours:** escalation decisions match after-the-fact
severity assessments; uses the full range of available responses
rather than always defaulting to the same one.
**Positive indicators:** explicitly weighs severity before acting;
adjusts threshold appropriately as new information arrives.
**Warning indicators:** consistently escalates everything ("cover
myself") or nothing ("don't want the hassle"); escalation threshold
unrelated to actual risk.
**Development potential:** trainable (feedback-rich, learned through
calibrated experience — the primary mechanism separating top-20% from
top-5%, Document 00 §2).

### B2. Judgement Under Incomplete Information
**Definition:** making sound decisions when full information is not
available and cannot be waited for.
**Why it matters:** most real security decisions are made this way;
waiting for certainty is itself frequently the wrong choice.
**Observable behaviours:** commits to a defensible decision within the
time the situation allows; explicitly names the assumption made when
information is missing.
**Positive indicators:** decisions hold up well under later scrutiny;
can articulate the reasoning, not just the outcome (Document 00 §2,
Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision framing).
**Warning indicators:** freezes when information is incomplete;
conversely, acts with false confidence and does not flag the
uncertainty to others who need to know.
**Development potential:** largely experience-driven; structured
scenario practice accelerates it but cannot fully substitute for
varied real exposure (Document 00 §4).

### B3. Risk Assessment
**Definition:** identifying, weighing, and prioritizing risks
accurately, including risks that have not yet materialized.
**Why it matters:** distinguishes reactive security work from
preventive security work; central to roles from patrol to executive
protection to risk management.
**Observable behaviours:** identifies multiple risk factors, not just
the most obvious one; prioritizes correctly when resources are
limited.
**Positive indicators:** anticipates second-order consequences of a
decision; revises risk assessment as conditions change.
**Warning indicators:** fixates on one risk category and misses
others; static risk assessment that doesn't update with new
information.
**Development potential:** partially trainable (structured risk
frameworks help); deep proficiency correlates with breadth of
experience across varied risk types.

## Domain C — Integrity & Ethical Conduct

*Mechanism: behavior under conditions where deviation would go undetected (Document 00 §2 marker 5; §5).*

### C1. Rule Adherence When Unobserved
**Definition:** maintaining the same standard of conduct whether or
not supervision or monitoring is present.
**Why it matters:** the clearest, hardest-to-fake marker of true
integrity; self-report is known to be weak here (Document 01 §5) —
behavioral and situational evidence are stronger (Document 04).
**Observable behaviours:** consistent conduct across supervised and
unsupervised conditions, verifiable via audit trails, peer report, or
incident history where available.
**Positive indicators:** self-reports near-misses involving their own
conduct, not just others' (Document 00 §5).
**Warning indicators:** documented gap between supervised and
unsupervised behaviour; history of successfully cutting a corner
without consequence (a strong predictor of recurrence, Document 00
§5).
**Development potential:** largely fixed as a disposition; training
can improve disclosure and reporting *behavior* around ethical
dilemmas but does not create integrity where the underlying
disposition is weak (Document 00 §4).

### C2. Honesty in Reporting
**Definition:** reporting what was actually observed, not what is
convenient, expected, or self-protective to report.
**Why it matters:** every downstream decision (investigation,
resource allocation, safety response) depends on report accuracy;
distorted reporting corrupts the whole system quietly.
**Observable behaviours:** reports include inconvenient or
self-incriminating detail when relevant; language is precise, not
vague or hedged to avoid accountability.
**Positive indicators:** corrects their own prior report when they
realize an error, unprompted.
**Warning indicators:** reports that are suspiciously clean or always
favorable to the reporter; vague language in exactly the places
precision would matter.
**Development potential:** largely fixed as a disposition; documentation
*technique* is trainable (Domain E), the underlying honesty is not.

### C3. Conflict of Interest & Boundary Awareness
**Definition:** recognizing situations where personal interest,
relationships, or incentive could compromise judgement, and handling
them appropriately (disclosure, recusal, escalation).
**Why it matters:** security and investigative roles routinely
encounter access, information, and relationships that create real
temptation; the competency is noticing and managing it, not merely
"not being corrupt."
**Observable behaviours:** proactively discloses potential conflicts;
declines inappropriate requests without being asked to justify it
extensively.
**Positive indicators:** raises boundary questions before they become
problems.
**Warning indicators:** minimizes or rationalizes boundary violations;
discomfort discussing the topic at all.
**Development potential:** awareness and disclosure habits are
trainable; underlying disposition toward self-interest is largely
fixed.

## Domain D — Communication & Interpersonal Influence

*Mechanism: the ability to transfer accurate information and influence behavior under normal and adversarial conditions.*

### D1. Clear Operational Reporting
**Definition:** conveying information accurately, completely, and in
the right format for the audience and urgency.
**Why it matters:** the bridge between what a professional observes
and what the organization can act on; failure here silently degrades
every other competency's value.
**Observable behaviours:** reports are structured, prioritized, and
free of ambiguity; adjusts detail level to audience (colleague vs.
management vs. external authority).
**Positive indicators:** anticipates the questions a report's
recipient will need answered and includes them proactively.
**Warning indicators:** reports that require follow-up questions to
understand basic facts; burying critical information in unrelated
detail.
**Development potential:** highly trainable (one of the more
responsive skills to structured training and feedback).

### D2. De-escalation & Conflict Management
**Definition:** reducing tension and hostility in a confrontation
while maintaining control of the situation and the professional's own
composure.
**Why it matters:** directly prevents escalation to physical conflict
or use-of-force incidents; central to public-facing and protective
roles.
**Observable behaviours:** uses verbal technique before physical
intervention where safe to do so; controls own tone and body language
under provocation.
**Positive indicators:** successfully resolves confrontations others
would have escalated; debriefs and learns from confrontations that did
escalate.
**Warning indicators:** matches or amplifies the other party's
hostility; skips available de-escalation steps.
**Development potential:** highly trainable (well-established
technique base; effect sizes for structured de-escalation training are
among the more robust in this literature).

### D3. Stakeholder & Cross-Functional Communication
**Definition:** communicating effectively with parties outside one's
own team — other departments, external authorities, the public,
contractors — whose priorities and vocabulary differ.
**Why it matters:** increasingly central at supervisor/manager/
coordinator level and in corporate, public-sector, and critical-
infrastructure environments where security must coordinate with
non-security functions.
**Observable behaviours:** translates security concerns into terms
relevant to the other party's priorities; builds working relationships
across functional boundaries.
**Positive indicators:** proactively coordinates before conflict
arises rather than only reactively.
**Warning indicators:** communicates only within their own function;
frames security needs in ways other stakeholders can't act on.
**Development potential:** trainable; strongly experience-dependent at
senior levels.

## Domain E — Documentation & Structured Thinking

*Mechanism: converting observation into a record and a decision structure others can rely on and learn from (Document 00 §2, marker 4).*

### E1. Documentation Accuracy & Timeliness
**Definition:** producing complete, accurate, timely written records
of observations, decisions, and actions.
**Why it matters:** documentation is a genuine safety behaviour, not
paperwork (Document 00 §2) — it is frequently how near-misses become
learning rather than being forgotten, and how legal/regulatory
exposure is managed.
**Observable behaviours:** records completed promptly, not
reconstructed later from memory; factual, specific, free of
speculation presented as fact.
**Positive indicators:** documentation quality doesn't degrade under
high workload.
**Warning indicators:** late, generic, or copy-pasted documentation;
quality drop is frequently an early, detectable signal of disengagement
or overload (Document 00 §5) — worth monitoring as a leading
indicator, not only judging retrospectively.
**Development potential:** highly trainable (clear technique,
checklists, and feedback loops available).

### E2. Structured Procedural Thinking
**Definition:** following and reasoning through structured procedures
correctly, including recognizing when a procedure doesn't cleanly
apply and adapting the *method* while preserving its *intent*
(Document 00 §3, adaptability-with-fidelity).
**Why it matters:** the difference between rigid rule-following (fails
novel situations) and structured thinking (handles them) is exactly
the difference between adequate and excellent procedural performance.
**Observable behaviours:** can explain the purpose behind a procedure,
not just the steps; adapts application to novel circumstances without
abandoning the underlying safety principle.
**Positive indicators:** identifies procedure gaps and escalates them
constructively.
**Warning indicators:** applies procedure mechanically without
understanding intent (fails novel cases); or abandons procedure
entirely under pressure (fails the "fidelity" half of adaptability).
**Development potential:** trainable with structured scenario-based
practice.

### E3. Analytical & Investigative Writing
**Definition:** organizing complex information (multiple observations,
sources, timelines) into a coherent, defensible written analysis.
**Why it matters:** central to investigation, intelligence, and
supervisory roles where the deliverable is understanding, not just a
log entry.
**Observable behaviours:** distinguishes fact from inference in
writing; structures complex material so a reader can follow the
reasoning.
**Positive indicators:** proactively flags gaps or contradictions in
their own analysis.
**Warning indicators:** conflates speculation with established fact;
disorganized material a reader cannot follow.
**Development potential:** trainable, more slowly than E1; strongly
correlated with Domain I (Investigative & Analytical Reasoning).

## Domain F — Resilience & Composure

*Mechanism: protecting decision quality and conduct under acute and chronic stress (Document 00 §2, markers 5–6; Crew Resource Management's "workload management" concept).*

### F1. Composure Under Acute Threat
**Definition:** maintaining functional judgement and controlled
behaviour during a physically or socially threatening confrontation.
**Why it matters:** distinct from general stress tolerance — this is
specifically about not freezing or escalating disproportionately at
the moment it matters most.
**Observable behaviours:** executes correct procedure while under
direct verbal or physical challenge; voice and behaviour remain
controlled.
**Positive indicators:** recovers quickly afterward without carrying
disrupted judgement into the next task (Document 00 §5).
**Warning indicators:** freezes, over-reacts, or shows prolonged
disruption to judgement after an acute incident.
**Development potential:** partially trainable (exposure/inoculation-
style training helps meaningfully); a real trait-based ceiling remains
(Document 00 §4).

### F2. Recovery & Emotional Regulation
**Definition:** returning to full effectiveness after a stressful,
traumatic, or emotionally difficult incident, and managing ongoing
emotional load without it degrading performance or conduct.
**Why it matters:** chronic, unmanaged stress load is one of the more
robust predictors of both burnout-driven attrition and the "drift into
failure" pattern (Document 00 §5) — this competency protects long-term
reliability, not just single-incident performance.
**Observable behaviours:** uses available support resources; performs
consistently across a demanding rotation rather than degrading over
time.
**Positive indicators:** proactively seeks support before performance
is visibly affected.
**Warning indicators:** performance/documentation/conduct degrades
progressively across a shift or rotation without the person flagging
it.
**Development potential:** partially trainable (structured recovery
practices, organizational support); underlying stress reactivity has a
trait component.

### F3. Adaptability Under Change
**Definition:** adjusting effectively to changing conditions,
instructions, technology, or role scope without loss of performance
quality.
**Why it matters:** security environments change (threat level,
technology, regulation, organizational structure) more frequently than
static job descriptions imply; rigidity is a real operational risk.
**Observable behaviours:** adopts new procedures/technology without
prolonged performance dip; reframes a changed situation quickly rather
than defaulting to outdated assumptions.
**Positive indicators:** treats change as information to incorporate,
not friction to resist (Document 00 §5).
**Warning indicators:** performance or conduct standard erodes under
change; resistance framed as principle rather than examined.
**Development potential:** trainable; correlates with Domain J
(learning agility).

## Domain G — Leadership & Team Coordination

*Relevant primarily, though not exclusively, to Security Supervisor, Security Manager, Security Coordinator, and other people-leading roles in the platform's scope.*

### G1. Supervisory Accountability
**Definition:** holding self and others to a consistent standard, and
taking ownership of team outcomes rather than deflecting.
**Why it matters:** a team's actual conduct standard is set by what a
supervisor tolerates, not by the written procedure — this is the
mechanism by which C1–C3 either propagate through a team or erode.
**Observable behaviours:** addresses substandard performance/conduct
directly and promptly; takes responsibility for team failures rather
than assigning blame downward.
**Positive indicators:** team members report the same standard applies
regardless of who is watching.
**Warning indicators:** tolerates known deviations to avoid
confrontation; blames individuals for systemic/team-level failures.
**Development potential:** partially trainable (structured leadership
training helps); underlying accountability orientation has a
disposition component (relates to C1).

### G2. Delegation & Resource Coordination
**Definition:** allocating people, attention, and authority
effectively across a team or shift, including under resource
constraints.
**Why it matters:** directly determines whether Domain A–F
competencies at the individual level translate into effective team-
level coverage.
**Observable behaviours:** matches task to capability; adjusts
coverage plans as conditions change during a shift.
**Positive indicators:** proactively cross-trains or builds redundancy
before it's needed.
**Warning indicators:** single points of failure in coverage;
delegation that ignores individual capability differences.
**Development potential:** trainable with experience and structured
practice.

### G3. Developing Others
**Definition:** actively building the capability of team members
through feedback, coaching, and opportunity, not just supervising
their output.
**Why it matters:** the primary organizational mechanism for moving a
workforce from top-20% toward top-5% at scale (Document 00 §2); a
leadership competency distinct from personal excellence.
**Observable behaviours:** gives specific, actionable feedback; creates
stretch opportunities appropriate to a team member's development
stage.
**Positive indicators:** team members' performance measurably improves
under their supervision over time.
**Warning indicators:** feedback that is vague, absent, or exclusively
corrective; no evidence of team member growth over time.
**Development potential:** highly trainable as a leadership skill.

## Domain H — Technical & Domain Knowledge Application

*Relevant across roles with a significant systems/technology/regulatory component: Security Technology, Datacenter Security, Aviation Security, Maritime Security, and increasingly all roles as technology adoption deepens.*

### H1. Security Systems Proficiency
**Definition:** effective operation of the specific technology
relevant to a role — access control, CCTV/VMS, alarm systems, SIEM,
or other domain-specific tooling.
**Why it matters:** technology increasingly mediates the vigilance and
judgement competencies in Domains A–B; poor system proficiency
degrades those competencies regardless of the underlying person's
capability.
**Observable behaviours:** uses system capability fully (not just
default settings); troubleshoots common issues without escalating
unnecessarily.
**Positive indicators:** proactively learns new system capability
without being required to.
**Warning indicators:** avoids system features out of unfamiliarity;
repeated basic errors.
**Development potential:** highly trainable; a knowledge/skill
competency, not a disposition.

### H2. Regulatory & Compliance Knowledge
**Definition:** accurate working knowledge of the laws, regulations,
and standards governing a specific role and environment (e.g.
aviation-security regulation, protective-security legislation,
data-protection law as it intersects with security operations).
**Why it matters:** regulated environments (aviation, maritime,
critical infrastructure, government) impose consequences for
non-compliance that fall on the organization and sometimes the
individual; this competency is a genuine risk-management function, not
bureaucratic overhead.
**Observable behaviours:** applies current regulation correctly
without being prompted each time; recognizes when a situation has
regulatory implications.
**Positive indicators:** proactively flags regulatory changes
affecting their role.
**Warning indicators:** relies entirely on others to catch compliance
issues; outdated knowledge unnoticed by the individual.
**Development potential:** highly trainable; requires ongoing
maintenance as regulation changes (feeds Domain J).

### H3. Domain-Specific Operational Knowledge
**Definition:** deep, applied knowledge of the specific operational
environment — e.g. datacenter cooling/power infrastructure basics
relevant to security decisions, maritime vessel/port operations,
aviation ground operations, executive-protection travel logistics.
**Why it matters:** generic security competence applied without
domain knowledge produces technically-correct-but-operationally-wrong
decisions; this is the competency that makes Document 07's
per-environment reasoning possible at the individual level.
**Observable behaviours:** decisions account for domain-specific
constraints a generalist would miss.
**Positive indicators:** sought out by colleagues for domain expertise.
**Warning indicators:** applies one-size-fits-all judgement across
clearly different operational environments.
**Development potential:** trainable but slow — substantially
tenure/exposure-dependent, similar to pattern-recognition expertise in
Document 00 §2.

## Domain I — Investigative & Analytical Reasoning

*Relevant primarily to Investigation, Intelligence, and Risk Management roles, and as an advanced extension of Domain B for other roles.*

### I1. Evidence Gathering & Source Evaluation
**Definition:** systematically collecting relevant information and
assessing the reliability of each source.
**Why it matters:** investigative and intelligence conclusions are
only as good as the evidence base and the discipline applied to
weighing source reliability.
**Observable behaviours:** distinguishes corroborated fact from single-
source claim; actively seeks disconfirming evidence, not just
confirming evidence.
**Positive indicators:** revises a working theory when new evidence
contradicts it.
**Warning indicators:** confirmation bias — stops looking once a
preferred explanation is "confirmed"; treats all sources as equally
reliable.
**Development potential:** trainable via structured methodology
(analytic tradecraft techniques); deepens substantially with
experience.

### I2. Pattern Recognition & Synthesis
**Definition:** identifying meaningful patterns and connections across
disparate pieces of information over time.
**Why it matters:** the core analytical skill distinguishing
investigation/intelligence work from single-incident reporting; also
the advanced form of Domain A's anomaly detection applied to
information rather than direct observation.
**Observable behaviours:** connects information across time and
sources that others treat as unrelated.
**Positive indicators:** patterns identified hold up under later
verification.
**Warning indicators:** sees patterns that don't survive scrutiny
(over-fitting); or misses genuine patterns present in already-
available information.
**Development potential:** slow to develop, strongly experience-
dependent, closely related to Document 00's Recognition-Primed
Decision framing — cannot be substantially compressed by classroom
training alone.

### I3. Hypothesis Testing & Objectivity
**Definition:** forming, testing, and being willing to discard
hypotheses based on evidence rather than initial instinct or
organizational pressure.
**Why it matters:** protects investigative and risk-assessment
conclusions from motivated reasoning, which is one of the most
consistently documented failure modes in investigation and
intelligence-analysis literature.
**Observable behaviours:** explicitly considers alternative
explanations before concluding; documents reasoning in a way that
would let another analyst check it.
**Positive indicators:** changes conclusion when evidence warrants it,
even under pressure to reach a particular outcome.
**Warning indicators:** conclusions that consistently match what was
expected or wanted from the outset; resistance to alternative
explanations.
**Development potential:** partially trainable (structured
methodology helps materially); resistance to motivated reasoning has
some disposition component related to C1/C2.

## Domain J — Learning Agility & Professional Development

*Mechanism: the cross-cutting construct from Document 00 §3 — not a performance competency in the same sense as A–I, but the mechanism by which a professional moves from top-20% toward top-5% over a career, and the primary lever for development recommendations (Document 08).*

### J1. Feedback Seeking & Responsiveness
**Definition:** actively seeking feedback and demonstrably changing
behaviour in response to it.
**Why it matters:** the single clearest behavioural marker of learning
agility, and one of the precursor behaviours that predicts long-term
success (Document 00 §5).
**Observable behaviours:** asks for feedback rather than only
receiving it when given; visible behaviour change following feedback.
**Positive indicators:** seeks feedback proactively, including from
people junior to them.
**Warning indicators:** defensive response to feedback; same
correctable issue recurring without change.
**Development potential:** trainable as a habit; some disposition
component (openness).

### J2. Self-Directed Skill Development
**Definition:** proactively building relevant knowledge and skill
without being required to.
**Why it matters:** the mechanism by which Domain H knowledge stays
current and Domain I expertise deepens between formal training cycles.
**Observable behaviours:** pursues relevant learning independently;
applies newly learned material to real work.
**Positive indicators:** learning is targeted at genuine capability
gaps, not just credentials.
**Warning indicators:** knowledge and skill visibly stagnate over
tenure; learning activity disconnected from actual role needs.
**Development potential:** highly trainable as a habit once
identified; correlates with F3 (adaptability).

### J3. Reflective Practice
**Definition:** systematically reviewing one's own decisions and
outcomes to extract lessons, not just moving to the next task.
**Why it matters:** the mechanism that converts raw experience into
the calibrated judgement described in Document 00 §2 — experience
without reflection does not reliably produce expertise.
**Observable behaviours:** conducts genuine after-action review of
their own decisions, including successful ones; articulates what they
would do differently.
**Positive indicators:** demonstrable improvement in decision quality
over time, traceable to specific past lessons.
**Warning indicators:** repeats the same category of error; no
articulated learning from past incidents, successful or not.
**Development potential:** highly trainable via structured reflection
practice (after-action review, structured debrief).
