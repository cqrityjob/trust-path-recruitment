# Document 07 — Security Profession Assessment Model

**Derives from:** [Document 00 — Security Excellence Model](./00-security-excellence-model.md) (each environment below is read against Document 00 §1's high-reliability-organization framing and §5's success/failure precursor behaviours), [Document 02 — Competency Framework](./02-competency-framework.md), [Document 03 — Dimension Framework](./03-dimension-framework.md).
**Scope note:** reasoning only — no numerical weights. Numerical Requirement Profile weighting is a Phase 2B/3 content-authoring decision within the locked architecture's existing overlay mechanism, not decided in this document.

## 1. How to read this document

For each environment, the question answered is: **given the same
master competency/dimension model (Documents 02–03), which
competencies differentiate performance most in this specific
operational context, and why?** This is not a claim that other
competencies are unimportant elsewhere — every foundational competency
(Domains A–F) remains relevant everywhere, per Document 06 §3's
coverage principle. What changes by environment is which competencies
carry the most *differentiating* weight for that context's specific
failure modes and consequence profile. This reasoning is what a future
Requirement Profile's numerical weighting should be built to reflect —
it is the rationale layer, not the number.

## 2. General Security (baseline operational, static/patrol roles)

**Most differentiating:** Domain A (Vigilance & Situational Awareness),
Domain B1–B2 (Calibrated Escalation, Judgement Under Incomplete
Information), Domain C (Integrity), Domain F1 (Composure Under Acute
Threat).
**Why:** this is the archetypal HRO monitoring role (Document 00 §1)
— long low-stimulation periods punctuated by rare high-stakes moments.
The differentiating failure mode is attentional lapse or miscalibrated
escalation, not lack of specialized knowledge. This environment is the
reference baseline the others are compared against below.

## 3. Datacenter Security

**Most differentiating:** Domain H1–H2 (Security Systems Proficiency,
Regulatory/Compliance Knowledge), Domain E1–E2 (Documentation,
Structured Procedural Thinking), Domain A3 (Environmental & Contextual
Awareness).
**Why:** the operational environment is technology-dense and
procedure-heavy, with tightly regulated access-control chains
(SOC 2/ISO-adjacent audit requirements are common in this sector) and
low public-facing interaction. Failures here are disproportionately
procedural/access-control breaches rather than physical confrontation
— documentation and procedural fidelity differentiate more than
de-escalation skill does, relative to General Security.

## 4. Critical Infrastructure

**Most differentiating:** Domain B3 (Risk Assessment), Domain H2–H3
(Regulatory Knowledge, Domain-Specific Operational Knowledge), Domain
F2 (Recovery & Emotional Regulation, given sustained high-consequence
exposure), Dimension D9 (Risk Perception) at an advanced level.
**Why:** consequences of failure extend beyond the organization
(public safety, national-security-adjacent impact in some sub-sectors),
and the operating environment typically has its own extensive
regulatory and domain-specific technical context (utilities,
industrial control systems). Risk perception must be well-calibrated
to genuinely severe, low-frequency events — a harder calibration
problem than General Security's more frequent, lower-severity event
mix.

## 5. Corporate Security

**Most differentiating:** Domain D3 (Stakeholder & Cross-Functional
Communication), Domain G (Leadership & Team Coordination, at
supervisor/manager levels), Dimension D12 (Interpersonal Influence).
**Why:** corporate security functions operate embedded within a
non-security organization and depend heavily on influence without
formal authority over other departments (HR, facilities, legal,
executive stakeholders) — the differentiating skill is less about
raw vigilance (though it remains foundational) and more about
translating security concerns into terms the rest of the organization
will act on (Document 02, D3).

## 6. Public Sector Security

**Most differentiating:** Domain C (Integrity, at heightened scrutiny
level), Domain H2 (Regulatory & Compliance Knowledge), Domain D1
(Clear Operational Reporting, given public accountability and audit
exposure).
**Why:** public-sector roles carry elevated public accountability and
transparency obligations; documentation and reporting must withstand
formal audit and public-records scrutiny in a way private-sector
reporting typically does not, and integrity lapses carry
disproportionate institutional and reputational consequence.

## 7. Executive Protection

**Most differentiating:** Domain F1 (Composure Under Acute Threat) at
the highest level in this model, Domain B2 (Judgement Under Incomplete
Information) under severe time pressure, Domain H3 (Domain-Specific
Operational Knowledge — travel/logistics/advance-work expertise),
Domain C3 (Boundary Awareness, given close proximity to principal and
their private information).
**Why:** the consequence profile is immediate and personal (physical
harm to a specific individual) rather than institutional, decision
windows are frequently very short, and the role requires sustained
extremely close-proximity trust — boundary awareness and discretion
differentiate here in a way that is less central to, e.g., Datacenter
Security.

## 8. Aviation Security

**Most differentiating:** Domain H2 (Regulatory & Compliance
Knowledge — a heavily internationally regulated domain), Domain A1–A2
(Vigilance, Anomaly Detection, in a screening/detection context),
Domain E2 (Structured Procedural Thinking, given highly standardized,
internationally audited procedure).
**Why:** aviation security is among the most externally regulated and
procedurally standardized security sub-professions globally; the
differentiating failure mode is procedural deviation or detection
lapse in a screening context, not judgement-under-ambiguity in the
way, e.g., Investigation work requires.

## 9. Maritime Security

**Most differentiating:** Domain H2–H3 (Regulatory Knowledge — port/
vessel-specific international regulation — and Domain-Specific
Operational Knowledge), Domain F3 (Adaptability Under Change, given
variable jurisdictional and operational contexts port-to-port), Domain
B3 (Risk Assessment across a genuinely international threat and
regulatory landscape).
**Why:** the operating context changes jurisdiction and regulatory
regime more frequently than most other environments in this model
(different ports, flags, and international waters), placing unusually
high demand on adaptability combined with deep domain-specific
regulatory knowledge.

## 10. Investigation

**Most differentiating:** Domain I (all of I1–I3, Investigative &
Analytical Reasoning), Domain E3 (Analytical & Investigative Writing),
Dimension D8 (Analytical & Systematic Reasoning) as a primary rather
than secondary dimension.
**Why:** this profession's core work product is a defensible
conclusion built from evidence over time, not a real-time operational
decision — the dual-process distinction in Document 03 §2 (D8 vs. D2)
is most pronounced here: investigation rewards the slow, thorough mode
that would be a liability in a fast-response operational role.

## 11. Intelligence

**Most differentiating:** Domain I1 (Evidence Gathering & Source
Evaluation) and I3 (Hypothesis Testing & Objectivity) specifically,
Dimension D8, and — distinctively for this profession — heightened
demand on Domain C (Integrity) given access to sensitive information
and the specific, well-documented risk of motivated reasoning
distorting analytical conclusions under organizational or political
pressure (Document 02, I3).
**Why:** intelligence work shares Investigation's analytical core but
adds a distinctive vulnerability — pressure (organizational,
political, or personal) to reach a predetermined conclusion — that
elevates I3's objectivity-under-pressure component above where it sits
for Investigation generally.

## 12. Risk Management

**Most differentiating:** Domain B3 (Risk Assessment) as the primary
rather than supporting competency, Dimension D9 at an advanced,
enterprise-wide-calibration level, Domain D3 (Stakeholder
Communication — translating risk into terms leadership can act on).
**Why:** this profession's differentiating skill is prioritization and
communication of risk across an entire organization's portfolio of
exposures, not detection or response to a single incident — a
strategic rather than operational application of the same underlying
risk-perception dimension General Security also draws on at an
operational level.

## 13. Crisis Management

**Most differentiating:** Domain B2 (Judgement Under Incomplete
Information) under organization-wide rather than single-incident
stakes, Domain G2 (Delegation & Resource Coordination) at scale,
Domain F (Resilience & Composure) sustained over a crisis's full
duration rather than a single acute moment, Domain D1/D3
(Communication, both operational and stakeholder-facing,
simultaneously).
**Why:** crisis management compresses several other environments'
demands into a sustained, high-stakes period — the differentiator is
less any single competency's peak level and more the ability to
sustain multiple competencies simultaneously (decision quality,
coordination, communication) without any one degrading the others,
directly the Crew Resource Management "workload management" concept
referenced in Document 00 §3.

## 14. Leadership (Security Supervisor, Manager, Coordinator — cross-environment)

**Most differentiating:** Domain G (all of G1–G3) as the primary
domain, Dimension D7 specifically, with Domain C (Integrity)
elevated in importance per Document 02 G1's finding that a team's
actual standard is set by what a supervisor tolerates, not the written
procedure.
**Why:** leadership is treated as a cross-cutting layer over any of
the environment-specific profiles above rather than a separate
environment — a Security Manager in a Datacenter still needs H1–H3,
but the *differentiating* skill at that level shifts toward G-domain
competencies, and the non-compensatory treatment of C1/C2 (Document 06
§7) matters more, not less, at this level given its propagation effect
on an entire team.

## 15. Cross-cutting observations

- **Foundational domains (A–F) never disappear**; what changes is
  which *additional* domains and which specific dimensions within the
  foundational set carry the most differentiating weight. A
  Requirement Profile overlay (locked architecture) is the correct
  mechanism to express this — it never modifies the baseline
  assessment, exactly matching this document's "reasoning changes,
  the model doesn't" principle (Document 01 §4).
- **Future professions** not explicitly listed here (the brief's "and
  future security professions") should be positioned by asking the
  same question this document asks per environment — differentiating
  failure mode, consequence profile, and operating-context demands —
  rather than requiring a framework revision. This is the direct test
  of whether Documents 00–03 were built at the right level of
  generality; if a new profession cannot be positioned this way
  without adding a new competency domain, that is a signal to revisit
  Document 02, not merely to add a row here.
