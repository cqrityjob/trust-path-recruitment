# Document 03 — Question Construction Principles

**Derives from:** [Assessment DNA — Science Philosophy](../assessment-science/01-science-philosophy.md), [Evidence Mapping Strategy](./02-evidence-mapping-strategy.md).
**Purpose:** permanent authoring rules every Question Library item must satisfy before leaving Draft status (Document 05), regardless of Taxonomy category (Document 01) or target Dimension.

## 1. Clarity

An item should be interpretable the same way by every respondent with
the relevant professional background. Ambiguity in wording is not
"realism" — a genuinely realistic security scenario can still be
described unambiguously. If two competent reviewers disagree on what
an item is asking, the item is not ready for Expert Review (Document
05), regardless of how well it otherwise reads.

## 2. Realism

Scenario content (Behavioural, SJT, Scenario-Based, Observation,
Decision-Making, Risk Assessment categories especially) must reflect
situations a professional in the target Role/Environment (Document 06)
would actually plausibly encounter — not a dramatized or generic
security trope. Realism is what supports face validity (Assessment DNA
Document 09 §2) and is a primary defense against the coachability risk
Assessment DNA Document 04 flags for SJT content: a scenario grounded
in genuine operational detail is harder to "game" from generic test-
taking strategy than an abstract or exaggerated one.

## 3. Security relevance

Every item must trace to a stated Competency/Dimension via the
Evidence Mapping (Document 02) — an item that is interesting,
plausible, or well-written but does not trace to Assessment DNA's
construct model has no place in the library, regardless of quality.
This is the item-level enforcement of Assessment DNA Document 01 §2's
"what should be measured" test.

## 4. Bias reduction

- No item may be constructed in a way that a specific demographic
  group would find systematically easier or harder to answer
  correctly/effectively for reasons unrelated to the target construct
  (the item-level enforcement of Assessment DNA Document 09 §5's
  content/construction bias concern).
- Scenario content must avoid embedding assumptions correlated with
  protected characteristics (names, settings, or situational details
  that inadvertently signal or advantage a particular group) —
  reviewed explicitly at Expert Review (Document 05), not assumed
  absent by default.
- Response options in forced-choice and multiple-choice items must be
  genuinely balanced in surface desirability (Document 01 §2.13) —
  an imbalanced option set is itself a bias-introduction risk, not
  merely a measurement-weakness risk.

## 5. Neutrality

Item stems must not signal the "correct" or "expected" answer through
tone, framing, or loaded language. A scenario written to make one
response option obviously virtuous and another obviously
irresponsible is not measuring judgement — it is measuring whether the
respondent can identify social desirability, which is a different (and
much weaker) construct entirely (Assessment DNA Document 01 §5 on
self-report ceiling effects applies with equal force to poorly
neutral SJT framing, not only to literal self-rating items).

## 6. Readability

Item language should be set at a reading level appropriate to the
target respondent population — clear, professional, and free of
unnecessary jargon, sentence complexity, or ambiguity that would
disadvantage a competent professional who is not a strong reader in
the assessment's language. Readability is a distinct concern from
Cultural Neutrality (§8) and from Accessibility (§9) — a highly
readable item can still fail either of those independently, and all
three must be checked.

## 7. Ambiguity avoidance

Distinct from Clarity (§1): ambiguity avoidance specifically concerns
response options and scoring keys — every response option in a keyed
item (SJT, Multiple Choice, Ranking, Forced Choice) must be
genuinely, defensibly distinguishable in quality/effectiveness from
every other option for an expert rater, with no two options that a
reasonable expert panel would rate as equivalent. An item where expert
raters cannot agree on the relative ranking of options is not
ambiguous in wording — it is ambiguous in construction, and belongs
back at Draft (Document 05), not merely a copy-edit fix.

## 8. Cultural neutrality

An item written for one market's cultural, legal, or organizational
context may not translate its underlying assumption correctly to
another (Assessment DNA Document 09 §7). Construction requirement: an
item's scenario should be checked for assumptions that are specific to
one country's regulatory environment, organizational hierarchy norms,
or cultural communication style, and either generalized or explicitly
flagged as market-specific metadata (Document 04) rather than silently
assumed universal. This is a construction-time discipline distinct
from the translation process itself (which happens later, at
Localisation, Document 04 §5) — an item that is culturally
market-specific by design should be labeled as such before
translation is even attempted, not discovered as a problem afterward.

## 9. Accessibility

Item construction must not create unnecessary barriers for respondents
with disabilities — avoid reliance on color alone to convey scenario
information, avoid unnecessarily complex visual/spatial description
where the same construct could be assessed through equally valid
text-based framing, and ensure time constraints embedded in a
scenario (Document 01 §2.5, Decision-Making) reflect genuine job
requirements rather than an arbitrary authoring choice that
disadvantages respondents who process text more slowly for reasons
unrelated to the target competency. Where a genuine job requirement
does involve a time or sensory constraint (e.g. rapid visual
anomaly detection in Observation items, Document 01 §2.7), that
constraint is itself part of the construct being measured and should
be preserved and clearly documented as job-relevant, not removed —
accessibility principles govern *unnecessary* barriers, not genuine
job-relevant demands, and the distinction must be made explicitly at
authoring time, not left ambiguous.

## 10. How these principles are checked

Every principle above is a checkable property, applied as an explicit
Scientific/Expert Review checklist item (Document 05), not a vague
aspiration. An item that cannot be checked against all ten
(§1–9, plus the Evidence Mapping traceability from Document 02) has
not completed authoring — this is the practical authoring-time
equivalent of Assessment DNA Document 06 §11's Blueprint-level
checklist, applied one level down, at the individual item.
